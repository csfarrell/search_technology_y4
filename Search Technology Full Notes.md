# Introduction
## Segment 1
### Definition of Information Retrieval
- Information Retrieval: Used to satisfy a users information need (number or name, explore new subject). Seeks to locate relevant content.
- Relevant content is what the user deems to completely or partially fulfil their information needs
- IR is concerned with unstructured information
### Approaches to Information Management
- IR systems seek to enable us to locate information relevant to our needs both efficiently and effectively. With IR, we can reduce user effort by directing users to potentially relevant material which might contain satisfactory information needs
- Information can be organized by flat collection of objects (files in directory, database records), or hierarchical (filing cabinets, sections, subsections)
- Physical organization depends on user context (can be solved by cross-referencing multiple contexts, links to possible related information, footnotes/see also)
### Approaches to Information Management
- Hypertextual linking: The idea of generalization, an extended form of cross-references, multiple info links and no superimposed hierarchy. E.g, links between wikipedia pages. This is bad a scaling however
- Links are limited by those which are added by the creator
- Web searching engines are used to context search as a means to locate relevant information at arbitrary positions within such structures
- Indexing documents have been used to locate relevant data since mid-1800s. This is slow and costly. Automatic indexing began in 1950s
- Why might automatic indexes be as good or better than human ones
- Archives may contain billions of documents and finding the ones which are relevant is difficult (needle in haystack)
- Search engines typically consist of about 2.5 words
- User needs have different forms of informational needs. Verification vs explorative, precise vs vague, shifting vs static topics
- Information needs may be addressed by: a single fact, single relevant document, multiple relevant documents, or by a regular feed of one or more updated relevant documents, for generative IR (content created dynamically for current query).
## Segment 2
### Search Requests vs User Information Needs
- Interaction: User interacts with system
- Stated search requests not the same as user information needs 
- Describing information in natural language is only possible with simple requests, can depend on context, which is usually not in search request
- Relevance needs to be judged in relation to information need NOT the stated search request
### The Searcher and the IR System
#### Cognitive View
##### Anomalous State of Knowledge (ASK)
- In an ASK, the searcher realizes that they have a need for information (complete some task or activity)

- ASK means user may not know or use language correctly to form search request which properly describes their information need.
- So the challenge for IR systems is that if users cant accurately describe their informational needs, then how can an IR system which is designed to return information which matches the contents of the query reliably resolve an ASK

## Segment 3
### Term Utility in Retrieval
- Key goal to devising most efficient IR system is to calculate the likelihood of relevance of a document to the users information need as accurately as possible for creating a ranked list
- How can we use terms to distinguish documents most likely to be relevant from non-relevant ones
- 1957 Hans peter luhn at IBM: Frequency of word occurrence in an article furnishes a useful measurement of word significance
- Karen sparck jones at cambridge: Terms present in less of the documents within a collection were deemed to have greater specificity or discriminative power
- Good indexing term will help discriminate one subset of a collection as relevant to a request
- Good indexing reflects relationship between individual document and specific collection it might be selected from
- Good indexing terms can contribute to the selection of the small number of relevant documents for a query from among the many non-relevant ones
- ![[Pasted image 20250930125814.png]]
- f = frequency of occurrence of words in a document
- r is the rank order (order of their frequency of occurrence)
- Zip's Law states: frequency(f) x rank(r) = constant
- Small number of words occur frequently (lack discriminative power) and a large number of words occur rarely (not sufficiently common to be shared across documents with common meaning)
- Good keywords occur moderate number of times
- Terms with maximum resolving power between the two frequency extremes
- Processing of search terms is often non-trivial
### Stop Word Removal
- Frequent words can be excluded from the indexing of documents without impacting on retrieval effectiveness
- The removal of common terms is referred to as stop word removal
- Stop word list is usually selected empirically for a particular IR system
- Stop words can vary between 10 and 500 words
- Removing them reduces computer memory requirement for a document by more than 50% and can improve search efficiency since there is no matching of stop words which a user has included in their search request
### Conflation
- Conflation procedure is designed to bring together words that are related to each other in some way
- Apply conflation methods to form standardised search terms
- Two main classes of conflation algorithms are string-similarity measures and stemming algorithms
- After conflation search tokens become search terms
### String-similarity Matching
- E.g Linear-Lynear, Sulphur-Sulfur
- Most matching problems can be addressed by string-similarity measures
### Affixes
- These are often removed to enable matching of different forms of semantically related words
- Examples of common affix types
	- Suffixes: carry-carries, forget-forgetting
	- Prefixes: cognition-recognition, compose-decompose
- These words are related by generally regard those differing in their suffix as semantically similar
### Stemming Algorithms
- Designed to remove suffixes that inhibit matching
- Operates by matching ending of a word against a suffix dictionary, removing any suffix identified, checking is there are context-sensitive rules that could apply
- Algorithm requires manual or automatic creation of a dictionary of suffixes
- In operation the end of each word in an input text or search request is compared against the suffix dictionary
- Approaching suffix comparison:
	- Longest-match: Word such as Alarmingly is stemmed to Alarm if both -ingly and -ly are in the stem dictionary
	- Iterative search: Word such as Alarmingly is first stemmed to Alarming by removal of -ly and then to Alarm by removal of -ing
#### Context-Sensitive Rules
- Its not always appropriate to apply stem removal
- Stemming is determined by the use of rules which examine the context in which the word is used
	- -ate, -ing not to be removed by create and sing
- Context-sensitive rules may list exceptions to the general stem removal rules (ie. do not remove stem in word sing)

- Recordings can be used to allow for changes in spelling that occur in addition to removal of a suffix in order to enable matching of different word forms. Invert and Inversion can appear just as Invert
- Manual evaluation of stemming algorithms suggest around 95% of words can be processed to give reasonable stems
- Changing stemming rules introduces different errors
- Retrieval where word stemming is employed is generally more effective but not greatly

	- What makes a term a good one is whether it can help to pick the generally very small number of relevant documents from among the many non-relevant ones
- Terms weights in standard best match ranked approaches combine several factors
	- Collection Frequency
	- Term Frequency
	- Document Length
### Collection Frequency
- Terms that occur in fewer documents are often more valuable than ones that occur in many documents
- Collection frequency weights are defined as follows for a search term t(i)
	- n(i) number of documents term t(i) occurs in
	- N = total number of documents in collection archive
	- cfw for term t(i)
		- cfw(i) = log(N/n(i))
### Term Frequency
- More often a term occurs in a document the more likely it is to be important for that document. Terms within document frequency
- Document frequency varies between documents
- Term frequency for term t(i) in document d(j)
	- tf(i, j) = number of occurrences of term t(i) in document d(j)

- Document relevance is independent of document length
- Term that occurs same number of times in a short document and in a long document is more likely to be more valuable for the former
- Longer documents will tend to have higher matching scores just because they are long

## Best Match Retrieval Example 8–10 — Document Vectors
Terms describe the topic of a document. The more frequently a term occurs in a document, the more this term tells us about the topic.

Each document is described by a set of representative index terms. Not all terms are equally useful for describing document contents.

Distinct index terms have varying importance. This effect is captured through the assignment of numerical term weights to each index term in a document.

Let *t(i)* be an index term in document *d(j)* and *w(i, j)* be a weight associated with *t(i)* in *d(j)*.

Each document can be represented as a vector:

### Link Structure of the Web - Background to PageRank
- Web pages: Number of forward links (outedges) and back links (inedges)
	- ![[Pasted image 20251020161035.png]]
- Can never know all back links but can know all forward links
- If A links to C, then A is indicating C is important with respect to A
- Links can be viewed as measure of importance
	- More links to page, more important the page is deemed to be
- PageRank provides a measure of this importance
- PageRank factors in importance of web pages which link to a page
- Value of link is dependent on PageRank of the page from which the link comes
- If page has important links pointing to it, it has higher PageRank value
- If A is deemed important (higher PageRank value) then it links to C is deemed important
- Actual text associated with a link is irrelevant in computing the PageRank score of a page
- Pages vary greatly in number of back links
- Generally page with many back links is more important than one with only few (not always true)
- Web page will have high PageRank score if sum of contributions from the PageRanks of its back links is high
### Definition of PageRank (1)

- Fi = set of web pages j points to (outedges)
- Bj = set of web pages that point to j (inedges)
- Nj = |Fj| = number of links from j

- PR(v) is PageRank of page v point to page j
- Nv is number of outlines from page v
- d is a 'damping' factor and is typically set to 0.15 (Google)
- Page v pointing to Page j, contribution to PR(j) depends on number of outlinks from Page v
- Calculating PR(j) if d = 1
	- ![[Pasted image 20251020161942.png]]
- Page A has 3 outlinks, PageRank is 0.6 since there are 3 outlinks it contributes 0.2 to PageRank value of each page it points to
	- PR(A) = 0.6, PR(B) = 0.3, PR(C) = 0.3
### Random Surfer Model
- PageRank can be interpreted as the likelihood that a random surfer visits a page
- Random surfer: Starts at random web page and keeps clicking and following links from page to page and not going back, eventually getting bored and jumping to start again at another random page
	- d can be interpreted as the probability that the surfer will move to a randomly chosen web page linked to the current page
	- (1 - d) is probability at each web page that the random surfer will get bored and move to random new page not linked to current page
	- If d = 1, assumed surfer will always follow random link from current page
	- If d = 0, surfer never follows link from current web page and all pages have PR(j) = 1.0
### Definition of PageRank (2)
- Computation of PR(j) using:
	- ![[Pasted image 20251020161737.png]]
- Requires value of PR(v) of each page pointing to it, hence the PR of every page pointing to every page
	- How can we calculate PR(j) if we dont know PR(v)
- Assume value of PR(j) for each page j
- Can compute revised value for PageRank of each page using equation
	- Assuming initial value of PR(j) = 0 for all pages and d = 0.15. PR(j) = 0.85 + 0.15 x 0 = 0.85 for all pages
	- The link structure becomes part of the calculation and each page j starts to have a unique PR(j) value
- Calculation of each PR(j) is repeated until the values of PR(j) do not change significantly after each recalculation
- Provided d is less than 1, convergence will occur
- Value we start with for PR(j), will end up with same final values or limiting values for each page
- Calculation can stop when value of PR(j) changes by just a small amount between iterations
- Calculation of PageRank value assumes that the set of documents is static (web is dynamic though?)

### Spam Filtering

### Link Farms
- Used to promote ranks of other web pages

- Link farms contain plausible pages which attempt to manipulate PageRank type measures of the pages that they link to by inflating link counts of target pages
- Merely exist to give high back link counts to target pages with intention of improving PageRank value and search rank
- Search engines seek to auto identify and block the influence of these artificial links
- Pages on link farms may contain
	- High density of outlinks relative to other content
	- Multiple links to same page or website from pages on the same site
	- Web pages containing nonsense text and many links
- Once link farm detected they are no longer effective, can be modified in some way

- This can go back and forward, known as adversarial search
- Could ignore content of the link farm and its links, but scores of pages linked to could be penalised

- Signals can be combined to give a composite score for each document

	- If trusted source
	- Technical issues/loading speeds

	- For each query obtain initial document ranking (e.g BM25)

	- If sufficient users click on document when entering similar query its assumed to be relevant

### Cascade Ranking
- Signal scores are calculated for documents in pipeline sequence of increasingly complex ranking functions
- At each stage of pipeline, lower scoring documents in ranked list are pruned and ranking the remaining non-pruned documents refined by incorporating next signal score
- Improvement in search efficiency when using Cascade Ranking can enable more complex scoring functions to be included resulting in improved search effectiveness
- ![[Pasted image 20251106132917.png]]
- The risk when using Cascade Ranking is that relevant documents are pruned if they dont score well using signals included so far at current stage of pipeline
- Pruning functions
	- Documents below fixed number of highest scoring documents in current ranked list are pruned
	- Fraction of current ranked list are pruned
	- Documents scoring below score dependent threshold in current list are pruned
		- < MaxScore − α × (MaxScore − MinScore)
			- MaxScore is max doc score
			- MinScore is min doc score
			- α is fixed scalar
	- As above but prune documents scoring
		- < MaxScore − α × (MeanScore)
			- MeanScore is mean of document scores
	- Documents score at each stage based on Learning to Rank type combination of signals at this stage
		- ![[Pasted image 20251107150537.png]]
	- Training of Cascade Ranking model similar to the single stage Learning to Rank model, except trade off between search effectiveness and efficiency
		- Higher efficiency (more pruning) may result in lower effectiveness (lower MAP)

- Let current system A be system integrating new ranking method B

- Some point both systems will run live

- Search engine provide can then evaluate features like number and type of interactions, how many returned documents are clicked, rank of clicked items and time between clicks

	- Do they appear more or less clear about what they're doing
	- Is average rank of clicked items higher or lower
	- Are searchers looking at more snippets before clicking (more or less work)
- Outcome of evaluation may not follow expectations of search engine provider
- Multiple examples of A/B testing producing counterintuitive/unexpected results

	- i.e. the user was observed to be working harder to retrieve relevant documents, and this version of the search engine might be judged to be better since they were more engaged with the content, and overall might actually succeed in retrieving a greater number of relevant items.

# Semantics For Search
## Segment 1
### Introduction
- Conventional IR by term matching is technically simple but effective which makes no attempt to understand contents of items being retrieved
- Can fail for various reasons
	- Mismatch between terms in the query and relevant documents
	- Ambiguity in query or document
- Conventional query and document matching relies on principle of redundancy of information in document s
- Type of redundancy is relevant information will appear in more than one document and at least one of these documents will use users query vocabulary and will return high rank
- Also, discussion of a topic in document uses variety of words referring to the same thing (writers encouraged to vary vocabulary to make it more interesting to reader), might match query
- This works on long documents but generally not short ones
- Search for relevant documents can fail when redundancy assumption fails
- Query expansion is one approach which seeks to address this, Semantic matching in search overcome the problem of mismatch by understanding features
- Text can be interpreted in multiple levels (words, phrases, sentences etc)
- Partial understanding can be used to better satisfy user information needs (address mismatch, form better summaries of retrieved information, provide answer to question type queries (QA))
### Sparse vs Dense Retrieval
- The size of vectors is equal to the vocabulary of the document size
- Each term in vocabulary can be represented by vector of same size within which the only non-zero element corresponds to the term itself (one-hot vector)
- A document/query can be represented by summing vectors of all terms present in the document/query
	- Similar to vector space model
- These vectors are referred to as observable or explicit representations since the representation features can literally be observed
- Vectors are generally sparse, since many have 0 elements, corresponding to vocabulary terms not present in the document or query
- Also considered high-dimensional since number of dimensions is equal to size of vocabulary
- Alternative to observable one-hot vector is to represent each term in different vector space
	- Use attributes based on distribution properties of the terms in documents
- Motivated by distributional hypothesis which states semantically related terms occur in similar contexts
	- Notion of distributional semantics is succinctly described in phrase "a word is characterised by the company it keeps"
- Set of attributes to represents each term based on contexts in which it occurs can be derived using ML process
- This process produces a vector expressed using these attributes
- Observations on these alternative vectors
	- Number of dimensions is much lower than one-hot vectors (100s rather than 100000)
	- Vectors are generally dense (most values are non-zero and real, not integers)
	- Attributes are referred to as latent since they are non-observable and have no simple interpretation (referred to as embeddings)
- Large corpus of text is analysed to create vector representation of each word
- Features of vectors are derived based on analysis of fixed size windows of words around each word
- Number of algorithms have been developed to efficiently create embedded representations from raw text input
	- word2vec method
### Word Embedding
- Word embedding algorithm creates fixed size vector representing properties of each word in terms of the derived vector features
- Vector representation of each word has size and direction
- Angle between vectors of the word is taken as the semantically similarity between words
	- Similar to Vector Space Model
- Elements of the vector for each word are trained using contexts in which the word is observed in the training text
- Each word represented by real value weights in the vector
- Elements of vector don't directly represent properties of word since they are trained automatically from large collections of text corpora by ML algorithm
- Attributes are latent or unobservable
	- ![[Pasted image 20251117193935.png]]
- We dont actually label the elements of the embedded vectors (royalty masculinity etc)
- Properties of these are each a dimension of the vector representing each word
- Illustrates importance of each dimension to the represent of the concepts of the word being described
	- King and Queen have higher degree of Royalty
	- Princess has lower age than King or Queen
### Word Embedding in Information Retrieval
- Each document and query can be represented as vector by summing the vector representations of each word
	- Possible since each word embedding vector is same size with the same representation features or dimensions
- As original vector space model the cosine similarity between vectors can be used as measure of similarity between the query and each document
- Use of low-dimensional dense vectors where all words are represented using same features in the same vector space means we have to overcome word mismatch problem
	- Related words will have similar vectors and high cosine similarity
- Concepts can match between queries and documents even if they dont contain identical concepts
- Simplest way to apply to IR query-document matching is to use cascade scoring model again
	- Use conventional search model like BM25 to create an initial ranked list
	- Create embedded vector for the query and compare with embedded vector of each top k (e.g 1000 ranked documents from this initial ranked list using cosine comparison) Either:
		- Rerank list using cosine vector scores
		- Rerank using combine score from initial pass and cosine vector score
	- Cosine vector score could be used as a signal in learning-to-rank method

	- Small evaluation dataset
	- Creation of LLM prompt

	- Only small amount of data required

	- One or more examples

### Improving LLM Effectiveness

### Complex Instructions
- LLMs find complex multi-step instructions especially if involves reasoning
- Can be addressed by breaking the instruction into simpler steps to be carried out
- Simplest version is chain-of-thought prompting which carries out a sequence of simpler instructions
- Has been recently expanded to more complex arrangements such as tree-of-thought and graph-of-thought where sequence of activities depends on outcome of previous instructions
### In-Context Learning
- LLM Performance following instructions can often be improved by including examples of similar tasks and expected output
	- Examples included in earlier prompt examples are examples of in-context learning
- Zero-shot learning: Prompts which there are no examples of requested activity

- Few-shot: Includes a few examples of requested activity
### LLM Fine-tuning
- General purpose LLMs can have poor coverage
- Fine-tuning involves small amount of domain specific data to adjust parameters of general LLM to increase coverage of specific domain
### Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
- More flexible way of improving domain coverage of general purpose LLM
- When LLM is prompted to carry out task, query is generated to seek and retrieve knowledge related to prompted task from external knowledge resources
- Retrieved knowledge combined with output created using the LLM to form overall task output
- Retrieved knowledge combined with output created using the LLM to form overall task output
- RAG is more flexible than fine tuning as it is when task is carried out
- More expensive at task execution time
### Hallucination
- LLMs make mistakes
- Hallucinations are when responses contain incorrect or misleading information presented as fact
	- Detecting is a major research challenge
- Occur due to errors in training texts but also since LLMs are encouraged to produce plausible responses rather than admit when they are not sure of something
- RAG can help address this

# Summarisation
## Segment 1
### Introduction
- Effective application of summarisation methods to the output of engines focus on information relevant to the searcher can improve search efficiency and user satisfaction
- Creation of document snippet is form of summarisation
- Key challenge is to determine what is in it
- For snippet in a SERP, content to be selected should be which is most likely to assist the user in determining the potential relevance of each document
### General Definition of a Summary
- Summary: Condensed derivative of a source text (through selection or generalisation of what is important in the source)
- Selection: Forming summary focused on subset of topical content of the source document
	- Subset detail, possibly same level of detail as the original document
- Generalisation: Forming summary which overviews the entire topical contents of the source document
	- Less detailed, higher level
- Summary usually has higher density of words relating to subject of document than original document
### Summary Utility
- Its hard to assess whether a summary will meet user needs
- Since much of the information is removed from original document in creating a summary by either generalisation or selection
- Selected summarisation process must seek to ensure that right information is retained in the summary to satisfy needs of the reader
- Considering requirements, need to be aware of:
	- Intended purpose of the summary
	- Needs of user
- Issues relating to purpose or scope of summaries
	- Function of summary (informing or alerting)
	- Who is the summary for (narrow or general)
- Must consider format also
	- Input document
		- Unstructured?
		- Structured?
		- Consistent format?
		- Structured is more complex to process than unstructured
	- Output (how should it be presented)
- Summariser is influenced by multiple factors
	- Form of input content (natural language, bullet points etc) Are these going to be preserved
	- Purpose of summary
	- Desired form of output (length, elements, screen space available)
### Multi-document Summarisation
- Multiple documents combined in a single summary document
- Documents are related are combined into single summary
- Challenges beyond single document
	- Identifying related material in different documents
	- Identifying unique information
	- Identifying repeated, contrasting, conflicting information and how to combine
	- Deciding information that should be included in the summary
	- Creating cohesive summary including selected information
### Human Summarising
- Consider how humans create summaries
- Strong emphasis is put on the purpose of the summary
	- People write summary for specific reason (scientific paper, event for newspaper, soccer match, business report)
- Number of stages
	- Identify key information
	- Create first draft
	- Several cycles of reviewing and redrafting to check information coverage, consistency and clarity
- Professional summarisers typically generate summaries to fit pre defined guidelines (formal/informal)
- Guidelines may describe
	- Desired style of language
	- Degree of reduction in text
	- Intended audience
	- Format of output
- Completion of summary may be guided by checklist ensuring guidelines have been followed and requirements met
## Segment 2
### Automatic Summarisation
- Seeks to replicate these processes
- Set up with a purpose or goal, desired length, overview or focus on some elements of document, expected form of input documents and required format of output documents
- Developed since 1950s
- Similar to IR, must consider summarisation in two phases, conventional text-level methods and emerging neural methods (LLMs)
### Conventional Automatic Summarisation
- Divided into two classes
	- Information Extraction and Synthesis
	- Sentence and/or Phrase Extraction
- Methods need to be crafted for specific types of input documents and required output type
- Instructions given to professional human summarisers will generally be too abstract for automation using conventional methods
- Since user needs of a summary and input and outputs of summarisation process are varied its not possible to develop single general purpose technology suitable for automatic summarisation tasks
- Range of summarisation techniques have been developed which are suitable for different situations
	- However even with task specific, creation of effective summaries still presents challenges.
### Information Extraction and Synthesis
- Information Extraction: Identifies and extracts key elements of information from a document and places them into a database
	- Info extracted from document using natural language processing methods
	- Extraction identifies entities (names, places organisations etc) relationships (actions, who did what, what happened etc), and uses those details to populate the database
		- E.g Dublin -> capital -> Ireland
	- Includes relationships between stored information elements
- Synthesis: Information from the database is used to create the summary as new text
	- Conventionally this involves the following processes
		- Text structure planning
		- Selection of information from database, natural language text generation
		- Contains information extracted from original text, but itself a new document
		- Conventional natural language generation can use wide range of technologies with different levels of complexity with differing levels of naturalness of the output. NLG can now include use of LLMs.
		- Can be used for multi-document summarisation
### Sentence and/or Phrase Extraction
- Summary composed of subset of the sentences and/or phrases taken directly from original document
- No generation of new text
- Generally analysis of original document is much shallower (easier) than for information extraction and synthesis approach
	- No attempt to understand/interpret text
- Method of this type used to generate snippets from SERPs
- Strategy
	- Score all sentences or phrases in document
	- Highest scoring as the summary
	- Consider context in deciding which ones
	- Summaries mainly consists of disjoint sentences/phrases from across document
### Summarisation by Sentence/Phrase Extraction
- Important sentences and phrases in a document are identified within the document and taken as the summary of the document
	- Each sentence may be scored using some metric indicating its importance in the document
	- Top n scoring sentences then selected or sufficient sentences taken to reduce the document to m% of original length
- These summaries generally lack fluency and can be difficult to read.
- Although the summaries are often not sufficiently fluent or comprehensive to replace document, can indicate whether original document will be of interest to user
### LLM-Based Summarisation
- Have been shown to be very effective at content summarisation
- They are instructed on the task to be carried out using  a prompt
- Outlined in that for development of ML in general, development of summarisation applications using LLMs is much easier than using conventional methods
	- The main requirement is to develop a suitable prompt (tell what it must do, give it input text, provide examples of input and corresponding expected output)
- Ensure that the LLM has access to information of domain of the content to be summarising (training, fine tuning or RAG)
- Prompt would specify
	- Length of summary
	- Style of text output
	- Format of output
	- Requirements relating to the reader (knowledge of the domain, reading/education level, age, etc)
- Multi-document summarising using LLM much more straightforward
	- Can handle the previously outlined challenges of LLMs without instruction

	- Use an IR system to identify small number of documents from within a large document collection which are potentially relevant to a request and likely to contain the answer
	- Attempt to extract the answer to the question from these documents
- Computationally attractive since cheaper IR component limits the information space over which deeper more complex and computationally expensive answer extraction component must be applied

- Typical workflow of QA system
	- Analyse request to determine type
	- If classified as question, determine type of question
	- Form search query from the question
	- Enter query into an IR system to retrieve documents which are potentially relevant many contain the answer
	- Enter output of question analysis module and top ranked retrieved documents into answer extraction module
- IR module would use enrichment methods such as query expansion to seek to improve retrieval of documents containing answer
- Answer extraction module analyses retrieved documents to try to identify the answer or answers to the question
- Then outputs potential answer/answers to question
	- IR component generally robust to different document types and domains
	- Answer extraction modules is more complex and less robust and so may need to be adapted to specific document types/domains/question types
- Retrieved document list is truncated either after fixed number of documents or if matching scores found to be low
- Tradeoff in precision and recall in deciding the length of the list: 
	- Too short: answers might not be included
	- Too long: too much non-relevant information may be included increasing the answer extraction cost and chance that the wrong answer is selected from non-relevant document
- Key feature of question answering component is ability to determine when it cannot answer the question
- Convention generally took one or two QA approaches
	- Knowledge-based: apply formal natural language processing and extensive linguistic resources
	- Data-based: Use large document collections with shallow informal language processing, exploitation of information redundancy
- Both approaches adopt the same standard QA architecture
- IR stage identifies a number of documents from which the answer might be extracted, differ fundamentally in the technology of the answer extraction module

# Recommender Systems

### Introduction
- Recommender Systems (RSs): Designed to predict items that may be of interest to user
- RSs and IR systems are similar as they both intend to identify items which may be of interest or use to a user
- Make recommendations on:
	- Profile of individual user and their feedback or "ratings" on previously viewed items
	- Feedback and ratings from other users
- Challenge is that reliable recommendations require large amounts of feedback on previous items
- Primarily used by merchants as method to increase purchase transactions
- Seek either:
	- To predict rating value that would be assigned to an individual item under consideration by the user
	- To rank top-k items by predicted rating values
- First case determining the absolute predicted value is important to determine whether to recommend item or not
- Ranking version of the problem is generally easier since only relative values of the predicted ratings are needed
- Goals of RS are generally:
	- Relevance: Recommend items relevant to the user
	- Novelty: Recommend new items relevant to the user which they have not seen before 
	- Serendipity: Recommend unexpected/surprising items which user finds relevant
	- Diversity: If diverse list of items are recommended, greater chance that one of them will be relevant to the user
- Difference between Novelty and Serendipity
	- Novelty: Items are new but previously unseen
	- Serendipity: Items are new and unexpected (not something that the sure will be aware of or previously considered)
- Serendipity:
	- Can be recommended based on choices of users with similar interests/purchase profiles as current user
	- Can increase sales diversity, but increases chance RS recommending non-relevant/annoying items to the user
- Generally found that the possible annoyance at being presented with non-relevant items is outweighed by user satisfaction at seeing relevant items of which they would otherwise be unaware of
### Collecting Data to Drive Recommendations
- Generally based on ratings of previous accessed items
- Ratings: How much does the user like this item?
	- Explicit: Actively provided by the user as their rating of an item
	- Implicit: Based on actions of the user (clicking, dwell time on item purchase, purchase of item etc)
- Similar to explicit and implicit feedback used in Relevance Feedback
### Categories of Recommender Systems
- RSs fall into three general categories
	- Content-based recommender systems (CBRS)
	- Collaborative Filtering (CF)
	- Knowledge-Based Recommender Systems (KBRS)
- CBRS Methods:
	- Analyse contents of a set of items rated by the user
	- Use contents of the item, as well as user's ratings of the items
- Information is used to infer user profile that can be used to recommend further items that may be of interest to the user
- CF methods use ratings from multiple other users with similar profiles to current user to make recommendations
- KEY CONCEPT: If users similar to you like an item, you might also like it too
	- Consider the identity of each item, but don't take account of contents of each item
- KBRSs method based on user interactions with RS to specify their interests
	- User specification is used with domain knowledge to provide recommendations
### Content-Based Recommender Systems
- CBRS recommends items by combining
	- Rating of previous items by user
	- Selection or purchase behaviour 
	- Analysis of the contents of items
		- John rates movie Terminator highly, metadata features of Terminator compared to features of other movies and ones which match are recommended to him
- CBRS essentially builds personal profile for current user based of features of items which have been highly related, based on principle that items matching the features of the profile should be recommended to the user
- Recommended items in CBRS are limited to those which match features of item in users profile
- System is only able to reliably recommend for a user once the user has made sufficient ratings of items to enable a meaningfully representative user profile to be built
- Key challenge of CBRSs is determining threshold over which items will be passed to user as recommendations
- Initially threshold is set with little information, over time more reliable threshold can be determined from larger amounts of feedback
- New items can be recommended without any user rating with the recommendation made based only on item contents
### Collaborative Filtering
- CF methods are based only on item identities, not on item content
- Provide recommendations based on ratings of items provided by other users who share common interests to current user
- Makes CF systems useful for recommendation of any type of item since they don't depend on content of the item
- Users profile in CF system is a set of items and their corresponding rating information
- Basic approaches to CF
	- Memory-based algorithms
		- Compute similarity between existing user and current user, select closest neighbours to current user
		- Items rated high by nearest neighbours recommended to current user
	- Model-based algorithms
		- ML methods to construct a model to represent behaviour of users to predict ratings
- Generally memory-based algorithms are simpler than other recommender algorithms
	- However more sensitive than model-based methods to common problems of RSs
	- Sufficiently large number of ratings of items by other users is needed to make reliable recommendations (model-based methods more robust to this problem)
- Since recommendations are determined by relationship between the users and the rated items user profile may contain multiple item types (books, clothes etc)
### Knowledge-Based Recommender Systems
- Knowledge-Based Recommender Systems (KBRSs) are useful for items purchased infrequently
	- Home, car, luxury goods etc
- Sufficient ratings are typically not available for these items to make reliable recommendations to a user using other RS methods
- Preferences may change over time
	- Specification of desired car likely to change between purchases (details of previous purchases are unlikely to be helpful)
- KBRSs make recommendations based on customer requirements and item descriptions, or use constraints to specify user requirements
- Knowledge-bases contain data about rules and similarity functions for matching process
- KBRSs allow user to explicitly specify what they want (price, make, model)
### Problems of Recommender Systems
- Sparsity of ratings
	- In most RSs, each user rates only small subset of available items, so most items remain unrated/sparsely rated
	- Makes reliable calculation of correlations between users and items they are likely to be interested in unreliable
- Cold Start Problem:
	- User-side problem: Difficulty of making recommendations for new users who have so far provided little rating information
	- Item-side problems problem: New items have not been rated often enough to make reliable recommendations
		- Generally will not be recommended often and so will not build up rating information
### Hybrid Methods
- Combine CBRS, CF and/or KBRS
- Provide better recommendations than any method in isolation
- Can be implemented in several ways
	- Making CBRS, CF and KBRS predictions separately and then combining
	- Unifying the approaches into single model
- Can reduce problems such as sparsity and cold start
- For item side cold start problem, CBRS can help provide recommendations based on new items content
	- However, cold start still a problem if user has not rated enough items to create meaningful profile
- KBRS can be used to specify the types of items that a user is interested in, which could be used as a filter for recommendations by a CBRS or CF
### Shilling
- Significant problem for RSs, shilling attempts to mislead RSs
- Effectiveness of RSs is dependent on amount and quality of the data available to it
	- Anyone can submit reviews
	- Motivations for submission of fake reviews/ratings:
		- Manufacturer or author may derive increased sales from over-positive fake reviews
		- Competitor submitting malicious reviews for competitive advantage
- Coordinated fake input to RS can disrupt predictions
- Single fake review may not have impact but coordinated attack can change behaviour of a RS
- Detecting shilling attacks is important in maintaining integrity of RS
- Trade of between precision and recall in detecting and removing fake profiles associated with fake reviews and ratings
	- Remove too aggressively real accounts will be removed
	- Remove too weakly and fake accounts will be missed
- Unsupervised: Determine characteristic differences between real and fake profiles, use these to detect fake accounts
- Supervised: ML used to train classifier to detect fake accounts based on examples of real/fake profiles
### Evaluation of Recommender Systems
- Requires
	- Set of items to be recommended
	- Ratings of the items
	- Set of users who made ratings
- In evaluation, RS seeks to predict which items would be rated as relevant to the user
- As evaluation of IR, set of items, user and recommendations, should be representative of the environment in which it is planned to operate the RS
- Depending on the task, evaluation should either
	- Test whether a specific item is correctly recommended to a selected target user
	- Measure the precision and recall of the top-k ranked recommendations
### Comparison with Information Retrieval
- IR systems respond to specific user search request to find items relevant to their current active information need
- CARS and CF:
	- Build standing profiles of user interests or ratings of items of interest to the user
	- Use these to make recommendations of items that user may find to be of interest
- KBRS are more like IR as they are interactive, but the needs are focused on precise user specifications and attributes of defined classes of target items

# Enterprise Search
### Introduction
- Enterprise search refers to application of search technologies to information within an organisation
- Biggest growth sectors for search technologies
- Enables search of structured and unstructured data in ways that support users and enable them to make faster/better decisions
- About 80% of enterprise content is unstructured
- Employees spend significant amount of time searching for information needed in their work (and often failing to find it)
- Key technical challenge for it is finding relevant information within its records
- E-information is usually accompanied by metadata, not found in paper documents
- The preservation of metadata from electronic documents creates special challenges to prevent spoilation
### Enterprise Databases
- Company information in databases:
	- Vital company data protected in transactional systems
	- Optimised for protection, not search
	- Skilled users with knowledge of database formulate search queries to extract relevant information when it is needed
	- Increasing interest in automatic transformation of natural language queries into database formatted queries
- Problems:
	- Queries to a database cannot be formulated if no one knows what they are actually looking for in the database
	- Someone needs to know what information is stored in database and how its structured, in order to make query for specific information
	- For search relating to legacy database possibly no one with knowledge of content or structure of database may still be working for the enterprise when the information is required
### Users of Enterprise Search Systems
- Enterprise search systems are intended for use within organisation by employees or authorities seeking information
- Information can be held in different formats and locations
- Searchers may be legal agencies in case of legal actions involving the organisation
- Public search services give the perception that configuring search systems has known solutions for all problems
- However enterprise search raises number of challenges not seen in web search settings
### Search Constraints
- Typically enterprise search does not make all of an organisations content available for search
- Constraints on content availability:
	- Considerations of security
	- Difficulty in indexing specialised content (video, images)
	- Difficulty integrating delivery of structured and non-structured content
		- Use single indexed file to search all content
		- Search different sources independently and merge separate results in the output
	- Sheer cost, time and difficulty to incorporate diverse content repositories held within an organisation into the search system
### Challenges of Enterprise Search
- Major challenges is the need:
	- To index content from wide range of sources within an organisation
	- To search indexed content effectively and efficiently
- Objective of output of an enterprise search system is generally to create an integrated ranked list from all sources which have been indexed in order of decreasing likelihood or relevance
- Users of enterprise search:
	- Class 1: Employees familiar with the information or documents that they are searching for
	- Class 2: Employees who may be looking for information held within organisation, but don't know exactly what is available/where it may be found
	- Class 3: Third parties (legal) who are looking for information relating to topic of interest
		- May have extensive knowledge of information or none
- Different users will have differing abilities of generating search queries
- Users will have differing abilities to recognise relevant content
- Metadata associated with information may have different formats
	- XML markup standards differ
	- Different metadata fields
- Means its difficult to index it in unified way within search index file while making effective use of the individual fields
- Varied nature of content means it can be difficult to produce document ranking
- Similar to web search it can be difficult to achieve and maintain coverage and freshness of contents of enterprise search system
	- Difficult to locate/extract all content within the enterprise, index and keep indexes up to date when content is revised
- Issues include
	- Presence of multiple duplicate content in different places
	- Near duplicate detection
	- Difficulty in determining content which has changed recently
- Successfully crawling content within enterprise can be very challenging
- Often there is no link-structure to assist with document ranking (cant use PageRank)
- Various signals can be used in query independent scoring of documents
	- Popularity
	- Recency of update
	- Spam score
	- Document type
	- Source repository
	- Unlikely to have sufficient data to apply click models reliably
	- Wide variety of queries associated with documents
	- Lack of redundancy in documents
		- Often relevant information is only in one document (right document must be retrieved, essentially this is high recall environment)
- Since its difficult to rank documents meaningfully, rich user interfaces can be used to enable exploration of collections
### Query Effectiveness in Enterprise Search
- Queries may fail due to differences between language of query and language of some or all documents
- Consider user classes
	- Users not familiar with organisation may not know its terminology
- Enterprise search systems may attempt to bridge the query-document mismatch gap using tools such as:
	- Stemming
	- Thesaurus expansion
	- Relevance feedback
	- Query suggestion
### Database Search
- Queries to a database typically use complex language form
- Requirement to know how database is structured
- Difficult to pose similar queries to multiple very different databases
- No ranking output from each database
- No meaningful way to integrate the output from multiple databases
- Where should user start looking among the retrieved content
- Database search similar to Boolean search in IR
### Modern Information Retrieval Search
- Characteristics of best-match IR
	- Queries can be posted in simple language
		- Although complex structures can be accommodated
	- No requirement to know how database is structured
	- Same query can be posed to any indexed document collection
	- Meaningful ranking of retrieved documents can be produced
	- Ranked lists can be integrated into single list (reliable merged ranking difficult)
	- User can start at top of list
### Comparison of Database Access and Search Technologies
- Database technology/systems
	- Users need training
	- Cant work with peak loads
- Search Engines
	- No training for users for best-match engines
	- Sub-second response time
	- Index can be updated in real time (nowadays)
	- Work with peak loads
### Database Offloading
- Offloading consists of:
	- Structured SQL queries applied to the database to probe what is contained
	- Each line of results retrieved from database in response to query becomes pseudo "document" which is indexed in search engine
	- Standard unstructured queries can then be applied to the indexed contents of the database to find out if it contains relevant information
- Database still exists, but its not the main access source for the information it contains
### Connection to Database
- Best-match queries can be used to search database
	- Connect directly to database:
		- Transform search request to SQL (or other native database query language)
		- Apply transformed query to database
		- Collect output
			- This means retrieved documents will not be ranked
	- Use search engine spider to crawl pages generated from database using database offloading
		- Index generated pages into standard text IR system
		- Apply best-match unstructured queries to data indexed in the IR system
		- Collect ranked output
### Access Control
- Key consideration is the right of access to information
- Different people have the right to see different things
- Controls can be used to limit access to those with authority to view certain information
	- Only human resources are authorised to access personal files
	- Only finance will have access to financial data
- Two classes of access control available
	- Early binding security
		- Access control attributes stored when document is indexed
		- Only authorised content for the current user is returned
		- More effort and storage required at index time, faster retrieval
	- Late binding security
		- Unrestricted search of content
		- Each entry in returned results list is checked at display time
		- Filters out entries that the current user does not have permission to access
		- Less effort and storage at index time, more effort and slower at retrieval time
- How fresh the content is
	- If its fresh, may be no opportunity to set controls at index time
- How quickly a system can respond to queries
	- Filtering at retrieval time can be time consuming/inefficient
### Compliance, eDiscovery
- Legislation requires organisations to store much of their internal information in case it is required for future legal disputes
	- Financial malpractice, illegal knowledge sharing, actions by or against employees
- Obligation to store all business communication
- Laws relate to businesses, health and civil organisations
- World post Enron
	- The Enron scandal (2001) exposed massive corporate fraud and corruption. Executives used off-balance-sheet special purpose vehicles to hide billions in debt and inflate profits, creating an illusion of financial health. When the deception unraveled, the energy giant filed one of history's largest bankruptcies. The fallout led to the collapse of its auditor, Arthur Andersen, and highlighted catastrophic failures in corporate governance, auditing, and regulatory oversight. The scandal directly spurred the creation of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act, which significantly reformed financial reporting and corporate accountability standards in the United States.
- eDiscovery: Discovery in civil litigation which deals with exchange of information in electronic format
- Usually digital forensics analysis is performed to recover evidence for use in legal cases
- Data is identified as relevant by lawyers
- Evidence is then extracted and analysed using digital forensic procedures
- Organisations only need to hand over information required by law
- Search can be used to cleanse content of other information which is not legally required before handing information over
- Individuals working in the field of electronic discovery commonly refer to this field as litigation support
### User Interfaces
- Web search engines are designed deliberately to be simple
- It needs to be simple so untrained users can use it
- Enterprise search systems typically give users much richer user interface functionality
	- Which occupy significant screen space leaving no room for ads
- The expectation for finding information in an enterprise should be fast and efficient, should be done in single interface
### Supporting User Needs in Enterprise Search
- More complex user interfaces in enterprise search allow for greater user control on output
	- Support methods such as cluster-based browsing and faceted search
- More complex interfaces may require the user to be trained int he use of the system and its interface
- Since search is part of their job, they will invest time to learning how to use the system
	- Even employee training course on it
### User Needs in Enterprise Search
- Users will often be looking for single known-item
	- They already know what they are looking for, but they need a search system to help find it
- May not be able to remember specific details needed to create a query which fully describes the document
- Search can be facilitated through exploration of available content based on what the users remember about it (referred to as exploratory search), rather than trying to find it in a single shot ranked output
- This search using partial memory of target item/items can be supported through methods including
	- Document Clustering
	- Faceted Search
### Document Clustering
- Documents can be clustered based simply on metadata fields
	- From the same email sender, written on same date
- Based on contents where documents with similar content (words, phrases) are placed into clusters as potentially related items
- Query based on what the user can remember can be used to direct them to document clusters matching their query
- Users can then browse clusters of related documents, rather than looking at separate documents in single ranked list
- Also supports other situations
	- User wishes to explore documents relating to project they are not familiar with, so are unable to create queries to find individual documents
	- Investigators are looking to explore documents related to some company activity
### Faceted Search
- Technique for accessing information by filtering items based on facets of the information
- Each facet corresponds to possible values of a property common to all objects
	- Author, language, format, data, source etc
- Faceted search can be useful where the searcher remembers one or more details of the item they are looking for but don't remember enough details to create meaningful search query
- Thus could start search by looking at items from something they do remember (e.g the person)
- Based on initial filtered set of items, user can select values of different facets to further filter the list of items and so on

# Multimedia Search
### Introduction
- Exists in many forms beyond electronic text:
	- Spoken content
	- Printed items
	- Static Images
	- Video
	- Music
- Extending IR methods for multimedia is therefore logical
- Information needed to satisfy a user need may exist in multiple media where the choice of medium depends on user context
- Conversely information may only exist in one medium
- Users may need to locate information across multiple media when researching
- Many items are multi-modal
	- The combination of different items makes the item fully relevant
- Combining information from different media can enable efficient search
- Many text IR techniques can be applied, which can be a challenge
	- Processing content to identify what it contains, enabling indexing of documents and interpretation of queries
### User Interaction with Multimedia Search Systems
- User interaction is crucial
	- Focusing on query expression
	- Engagement with results
	- Satisfaction of information needs
- Important in multimedia IR where information is presented in different forms, with temporal dimension for audio and video
- Human interaction differs by medium
	- Text: Must be read but can be annotated for navigation
	- Images: Information can be grasped quickly, multiple images can be browsed rapidly as thumbnails
	- Spoken Content: Temporal, can only be accessed in real-time. Transcripts are an option but have drawbacks
	- Video: Temporal but can be summarised via representative keyframes, allowing rapid visual browsing
- Advances enable non-text or multi-modal queries
- Information is expressed differently across media, affecting both technical solutions and perceived usefulness
### Querying Multimedia Search Systems
- Multimedia search requests, while seeming simple, can be complex for machines to interpret
	- E.g: "Find movies with car chases", "Find goals in soccer matches", "Find tries by Johnny Sexton"
	- Within specific video: "Show me the car chase in this movie", "Show me a summary focusing on Lewis Hamilton", "query by example" using an image
### Content Recognition
- Speech: Use Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) or crowdsourcing (Amazon Mechanical Turk), though AST is imperfect and confidential content limits crowdsourcing
- Printed/Handwritten Content: Optical Character Recognition (OCR) or crowdsourcing, accuracy is a challenge however
- Images & Video: Requires analysis to identify useful features (objects, scenes, events)
	- Automatic analysis or crowdsourced labelling can be used. Video adds challenge of temporal event detection
- Music: Involves extracting features (melody, chords, lyrics, instruments). Analysis of audio is difficult, analysis of printed notation similar to OCR
### Interacting with Retrieved Results
- Major challenge is representing retrieved content for relevance judgement and providing efficient access
	- Representation: Thumbnails, keyframes, audio snippets
	- Access: Listening to or watching entire items is time-consuming. System must help user locate specific sections quickly
### Scope of Multimedia Search
- Multimedia Information Retrieval (MIR) is actively being researched and seeks to understand user needs and develop effective technologies for recognition, indexing, query analysis and result interaction.
- Solutions are not definitive
### Searching Multimedia using Text
- Text multimedia accompanying multimedia content is often used for search.
- Sources include alt text, URLs, nearby text, anchor text, document headings
- Metadata can be manually created, automated, or crowdsourced
- Limitations include:
	- Limited value for temporal media (entire item may need review)
	- Very limited descriptions of actual content
	- Failure to locate relevant content within a file
- Metadata can be enriched using knowledge graphs or context data
- Text IR can be combined with metadata filtering
- Annotations can also be expanded using resources like Wikipedia
### Multimodal Information Retrieval
- While text based retrieval enables some access, its limited
- More powerful search is based on actual multimedia content.
- Maximum effectiveness is often achieved by combining content-based information with text metadata
### Retrieval of Spoken Content
- Spoken Content Retrieval (SCR) scenarios include 
	- Text queries over spoken data
	- Spoken queries over spoken data
- Ideal approach: Creating perfect transcript and applying text IR
	- However this is not feasible to
		- Imperfect ASR output
		- Uneconomic manual transcription
		- Variable quality of crowdsourcing
- SCR also faces challenges with long, multi-topic content making audition inefficient and ranking unreliable.
- Key questions are
	- What should retireval units be
	- How are they identified
- Using error-full AST transcripts for indexing reduces retrieval effectiveness
	- Measured by metrics like
		- Percision
		- Recall
		- MAP
- User evaluation is essential to determine if a system with reduced performance is still acceptable
### ASR Output and Errors
- ASR errors are classified as
	- Substitutions: Spoken word is recognised as different word
	- Deletions: Spoken word is omitted
	- Insertions: Word not spoken is added
- Errors worsen with Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) words
- Early SCR focused on "easy" domains like broadcast news (clear documents, well-trained AST)
- More challenging scenarios involve non-self-describing content (requires enriched metadata) or long unstructured audio without clear boundaries, making segmentation difficult
### Accessing and Browsing Spoken Content
- Temporal nature of audio makes browsing inefficient
- Speech can be understood at ~2x speed, it is cognitively demanding
- Therefore systems must
	- Identify suggested "jump-in" points for playback
	- Provide sufficient context
	- Use visual interfaces to represent content
- Presenting ASR transcripts is one option (e.g Scammail system)
- Studies show transcripts with Word Error Rate (WER) < 25% are useful, while those with WER > 45% are unsatisfactory
- Crucially users must not develop unfounded trust in transcripts
	- This can cause them to miss information absent due to ASR errors
- Visual browsing tools represent audio as static images/timeline.
	- Video Mail Retrieval (VMR): Shows query words on a timeline, with brightness indicating ASR confidence
	- Kunststofzuiger Podcast Search: Shows a term cloud and query word markers on a player timeline
	- VMR News Browser: Uses a heat map where segment brightness indicates relevance to the query
	- Radio Oranje App: Uses magnifying glass metaphor to show overview and a detailed, transcript-synced view
### Spoken Input to Applications & Multi-modal Interaction on Mobile Devices
- Cloud-based ASR enables robust "Search by Voice" on mobile devices
	- Attractive where text input is inconvenient
- However its unsuitable in noisy/private settings
- Mobile devices support rich multi-modal interaction (input: text, speech, touch. Output: text, graphics, video)
- Visual output is generally richer and more efficient than audio
	- Offers greater bandwidth
	- Reduced cognitive load
	- Re-scannability (Google maps)
- Audio output is preferred for "eyes-busy" tasks or users with visual impairments
### Content-Based Retrieval of Visual Media
- Poses significant challenges
	- How to represent content
	- What features to identify
	- How should queries be posed (text vs example image)
	- User queries are often incomplete/weakly specified
	- Visual media is content-rich with many possible interpretations
- A "human-in-the-loop" approach allows iterative refinement of the information need
### The Semantic Gap
- The difference between low-level multimedia data and its human-interpreted meaning
	- Early work used low-level features (colour, texture)
		- Large gap
	- Later work used detectors for specific objects/people
		- Smaller gap
	- Domain-specific high-level models could interpret combinations of features as activities, but required pre-specification
### Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs)
- Revolutionising the field by extending LLMs to process visual and audio data
- Trained on massive multimodal datasets and can
	- Recognise and categorise images/videos with high accuracy without pre-built detectors
	- Generate detailed descriptions from media and vice-versa
	- Analyse temporal events in video and audio
	- Perform cross-media transformations and joint analysis of multiple media
- Significantly reduces semantic gap
	- Enables near-human interpretation and powerful advanced applications like multimodal QA and summarisation
- However, fundamental issues of human-media engagement remain
### Content-Based Retrieval of Video
- Video adds a temporal dimension and is often combined with audio
- While metadata search is limited, content-based search is more powerful
- Video is structured as Scenes composed of Shots (continuous frames from one camera)
- Keyframes (middle frame of shot, selected via MLLM) can represent shots for browsing
### Video Search
- Query methods include
	- Text queries returning ranked shots
	- Text & feature filters
	- Query-by-example using keyframe or image
	- Object Search
	- Spatiotemporal (both space and time) queries (green car moving towards camera)
- Video retrieval is typically two-phase process combining searching to narrow options and browsing to find precise content, facilitated by the "human-in-the-loop"
### Combining Content and Context: Personal Digital Photo Search with MediaAssist
- Effective systems creatively combine technologies
- MediaAssist system for personal photo search demonstrates this by fusing
	- Content: Face detection/recognition (improved via user feedback)
	- Context: GPS location, time/place, calculated ambient light and archived weather data
	- Structure: Automatic grouping of photos into "events" based on spatiotemporal proximity
- This integration allows for powerful and intuitive search queries based on human memory
